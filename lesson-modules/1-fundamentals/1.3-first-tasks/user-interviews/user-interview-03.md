# User Interview #3: Recent Graduate / First-Time Budgeter Persona

**Participant:** Kenji Watanabe
**Role:** Junior Marketing Coordinator
**Age:** 23
**FinTrack Plan:** Monthly ($9.99/mo)
**Date:** February 7, 2025
**Interviewer:** Senior PM
**Duration:** 26 minutes

---

## Background

Kenji graduated last May and started his first full-time job in September. He's living alone for the first time, paying rent and utilities on his own. He tried budgeting in a notebook for the first two months and gave up. A TikTok about personal finance led him to download FinTrack three months ago.

---

## Interview

**Q: What was your relationship with money before FinTrack?**

Kenji: "Basically nonexistent planning-wise. I knew my salary and I'd check my bank account to make sure I had enough before buying stuff. That was it. I had no idea what I was spending on food versus going out versus whatever. I'd always be surprised by how much my credit card balance was at the end of the month."

**Q: What was the onboarding experience like?**

Kenji: "It was actually pretty easy to connect my accounts. That part surprised me - I expected it to be complicated. The app walked me through it step by step. The first time I saw all my transactions automatically categorized was kind of mind-blowing. Like, I never knew I was spending $340 a month on restaurants until it was just... there."

**Q: How has FinTrack changed your habits?**

Kenji: "I think twice before eating out now. Which sounds small but it adds up. I can see in real time how close I am to my dining budget. I've probably saved $100-150 a month just from being more aware. It's not that I'm obsessing over it - just having the number visible changes the behavior."

**Q: What's been confusing or frustrating?**

Kenji: "The categories. I get why they exist but sometimes I don't know where something should go. Like, I bought a book - is that 'education' or 'shopping'? And the AI will sometimes put things in weird places. My gym membership got put under 'health' one month and 'personal care' another month. I want it to be consistent."

**Q: How do you use the app day to day?**

Kenji: "Almost entirely on my phone. I basically never use the desktop version. I check the spending overview when I'm curious, usually right after I buy something. The transaction feed is where I spend most of my time. I don't use the budget goals much to be honest - I set a few but I don't check them as regularly as I probably should."

**Q: What would make FinTrack more useful for you as someone who's new to budgeting?**

Kenji: "More guidance built into the app. Like, when I first signed up it just said 'set your budget' but I had no idea what realistic amounts were. How much should a 23-year-old making $52k actually budget for groceries? For going out? Some kind of benchmarks or starter guidance would have been really helpful. Other apps I looked at had these little insights like 'you spend more on coffee than 80% of users your age.'"

**Q: What about the credit score monitoring?**

Kenji: "That's actually been useful. I didn't know what my credit score was before. Now I check it in the app and I learned that I needed to lower my credit utilization. My score went up 40 points in two months just from following the tips. That feels like a real win."

**Q: Is there anything you'd pay more for?**

Kenji: "I'm kind of a tight budget so I'm already stretching a bit at $9.99. If they offered like a student tier or a cheaper option with less features I might do that. The credit score thing and the spending tracking are probably the two features I actually use regularly. Don't need everything fancy."

**Q: What would make you recommend FinTrack to friends?**

Kenji: "If it gave more personalized advice for people just starting out. A lot of my friends from college are in the same situation - first job, first apartment, no clue about money. The app works, but it feels like it's designed for people who already know what they're doing."

---

## Key Insights
- Automatic transaction display is the "aha" moment for new budgeters
- Spending awareness alone drives behavior change (no active effort required)
- AI categorization inconsistency is confusing for users who don't have strong mental models
- Mobile-first usage pattern - desktop rarely opened
- Budgeting benchmarks/guidance needed for first-time users (no baseline knowledge)
- Credit score monitoring is a high-value hook for younger demographic
- Price sensitivity is real - potential for lower-tier or student plan
- Onboarding friction is low, but post-setup guidance is lacking
