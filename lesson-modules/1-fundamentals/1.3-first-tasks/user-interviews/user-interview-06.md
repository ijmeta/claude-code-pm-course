# User Interview #6: Near-Retirement Persona

**Participant:** Robert Stafford
**Role:** Regional Sales Manager
**Age:** 52
**FinTrack Plan:** Annual ($79/yr)
**Date:** February 14, 2025
**Interviewer:** Senior PM
**Duration:** 36 minutes

---

## Background

Robert has been with the same company for 18 years. He and his wife have two grown kids and a mortgage that's about 8 years from being paid off. He's been using FinTrack for about 6 months after his financial advisor recommended getting a clearer picture of his overall net worth. He's focused on retirement planning and wants to know if he's on track.

---

## Interview

**Q: What was your financial picture before FinTrack?**

Robert: "Scattered. I knew my 401k balance. I had a brokerage account I barely looked at. My wife manages the day-to-day spending. I had a general sense of things but no consolidated view. My financial advisor would ask me what my net worth was and I'd have to guess."

**Q: What made you decide to use FinTrack now, at this point in your life?**

Robert: "Honestly, I started doing the math on retirement and got nervous. Am I on track? When can I retire? I realized I had no idea. I was just contributing the max to my 401k and hoping for the best. My advisor said I should get everything in one place so we could have a real conversation."

**Q: How has FinTrack helped with that?**

Robert: "The net worth tracker is the main thing I use. Every account in one dashboard - checking, savings, the 401k, the brokerage account, the house equity, the mortgage balance. Seeing it all together gave me a real number for the first time. I'm actually in better shape than I thought, which was a relief."

**Q: Walk me through your typical week with FinTrack.**

Robert: "I probably check it once a week. I look at the net worth trend - is it going up? I check the investment accounts to see if anything needs attention. I look at our household spending to make sure nothing weird is happening. My wife handles the budget day-to-day, but I like to know the big picture."

**Q: What are you most frustrated by?**

Robert: "The investment tracking is the weakest part for me. My 401k shows up as a lump sum but I can't see the allocation breakdown - how much is in equities vs bonds vs cash. My brokerage account shows my holdings but no performance benchmarking. Like, am I beating the S&P 500 or not? That's the number I care about. I'd have to export data and calculate it myself, which defeats the purpose."

**Q: Tell me more about what investment tracking would ideally look like.**

Robert: "I want to see my total portfolio allocation - stocks vs bonds vs cash - across all accounts. I want a return calculation that shows me my actual performance versus a benchmark. And I want a retirement projection - if I keep saving at this rate, when can I retire with 80% income replacement? FinTrack has the data but it doesn't do this analysis. I'm still going to spreadsheets for this."

**Q: How about the banking side of things?**

Robert: "Rock solid. Never had an issue with my connections. Checking, savings, the mortgage - all stay connected. I don't track spending as obsessively as maybe a younger person would, but it's good to have it there. We did find two subscriptions we forgot about - that was a nice surprise."

**Q: What about your wife? Does she use FinTrack too?**

Robert: "She looks at the app sometimes but it's really my account. Which is a little awkward - she manages the grocery budget and the household stuff but she doesn't have a real role in the app. She'd probably use it more if it felt like a joint account rather than Robert's account she's allowed to see."

**Q: What features would make FinTrack significantly more valuable for someone near retirement?**

Robert: "A retirement readiness score, honestly. Tell me if I'm on track or not, in plain English. Something like: 'At your current savings rate, you can retire comfortably at 63.' That's the number I want. Everything else is secondary. And better investment analysis - I pay my financial advisor partly because FinTrack doesn't do the portfolio analysis I need."

**Q: How do you feel about the annual price?**

Robert: "I'd pay more if the investment and retirement features were better. $79 is nothing for what it does now. If it replaced even half of what I use my financial advisor for, I'd pay $200 a year without blinking."

---

## Key Insights
- Net worth consolidated view is primary value driver for users nearing retirement
- Investment tracking is significantly underpowered - missing allocation breakdown, performance benchmarking
- Retirement projection / readiness score is a highly desired but absent feature
- Bank sync reliability is excellent - no complaints for this segment
- Household joint account dynamic is awkward for couples where one person manages finances
- Willingness to pay is high if investment/retirement features improve
- Compares FinTrack to financial advisor - potential to displace some advisor functions
- Once-per-week usage pattern is normal for this segment; not daily budgeters
