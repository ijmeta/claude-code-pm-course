# User Research: Personal Finance Pain Points

**Research Date:** December 2024
**Method:** 20 in-depth user interviews (45–60 min each)
**Participants:** Current FinTrack users across young professionals, families, and debt payoff users

---

## Key Themes

### 1. "I Don't Know Where My Money Goes"

**What we heard:**

> "I make decent money but I have no idea why I'm not saving more. Every month I look at my bank account and think — where did it all go?" — Jordan, 29, software engineer

> "I could tell you roughly what I spend on rent and groceries. Beyond that? Complete mystery. I had no idea I was spending $400 a month on dining out until FinTrack showed me." — Marketing manager, 31

> "My wife and I both have jobs but we still feel broke. We have no shared visibility into where the household money is actually going." — Husband, 35, one of two earners in household

**Pain points:**
- No real-time visibility into spending across all accounts
- Money disappears into subscriptions, dining, and impulse purchases without awareness
- Joint households with separate accounts have no combined picture
- Monthly credit card statements arrive too late to course-correct

---

### 2. Automatic Bank Sync Breaks Too Often

**What we heard:**

> "I set it up once and it worked for two months, then my Chase account just... disconnected. I didn't notice for three weeks. By then I had a month of uncategorized transactions." — Alex, 34, marketing manager

> "I have six accounts — checking, two credit cards, a savings, a 401k, and a brokerage. At any given time at least one of them is showing a sync error. It's exhausting." — Freelancer, 38

> "When the sync breaks and I have to manually reconnect the bank, it feels like a punishment. I'm already trying to do the right thing — don't make it harder." — Parent, 41

**Pain points:**
- Bank connections drop after password changes, MFA updates, or bank-side changes
- Reconnection flow is confusing and time-consuming
- Multiple accounts means multiple potential failure points
- Trust erodes when the "automatic" product requires manual intervention

---

### 3. AI Categorization Is Good But Not Perfect

**What we heard:**

> "It gets most things right but there are a few vendors it always miscategorizes. My gym membership keeps showing up as Healthcare. I've corrected it five times." — Young professional, 27

> "Amazon is impossible. Is it household supplies? Entertainment? Electronics? It categorizes everything differently and I can't figure out the logic." — Parent, 33

> "The categorization is way better than doing it manually, but when it's wrong and I don't catch it, my budget numbers are garbage." — PM, 29

**Pain points:**
- Recurring vendors sometimes miscategorized repeatedly despite corrections
- Amazon, Venmo, and PayPal are especially hard to categorize accurately
- Errors compound — one miscategorized transaction distorts the budget view
- Users who care about accuracy spend time auditing, which defeats the "automated" promise

---

### 4. Investments Are Invisible

**What we heard:**

> "I have a 401k and a Vanguard brokerage account. They're not in FinTrack. So my 'net worth' shows me as basically broke because it's just checking and savings." — Engineer, 32

> "The budgeting stuff is great. But I can't see my full financial picture without my investments. I end up using two apps — FinTrack for spending and Empower for investments." — Consultant, 36

> "My biggest financial asset is my retirement account and FinTrack acts like it doesn't exist. That's kind of a big miss." — Mid-career professional, 44

**Pain points:**
- Investment and retirement accounts not synced in most configurations
- Net worth tracker feels incomplete without brokerage/401k
- Users running two apps (FinTrack + Empower or Copilot) instead of one
- Particularly frustrating for users who chose FinTrack as their "one app"

---

### 5. Budgeting Feels Too Manual to Maintain

**What we heard:**

> "I set up my budget in January. By March I stopped looking at it because the categories didn't match how I actually spend." — User, 28

> "The tool is great but I have to remember to check it. It doesn't come to me. I wish it would just tell me when I'm off track instead of waiting for me to log in." — Busy parent, 38

> "I love the concept but the setup takes forever if you want it to actually reflect your life. And then you have to update it every time something changes." — Freelancer, 31

**Pain points:**
- Initial budget setup is time-consuming, especially for first-time budgeters
- Budgets go stale as spending patterns change (new job, new rent, new bills)
- App is passive — users must come to it rather than it surfacing insights proactively
- Motivation fades when the budget doesn't feel relevant to actual behavior

---

### 6. No Coaching or Next Steps

**What we heard:**

> "FinTrack shows me the numbers but it doesn't tell me what to do about them. I see that I'm overspending on dining — now what?" — Young professional, 26

> "I know my savings rate is 9%. I know 20% is better. But the app doesn't help me figure out how to get from here to there." — Engineer, 30

> "I want the app to be like a financial advisor that actually knows my numbers. Not a dashboard I have to interpret myself." — Marketing manager, 34

**Pain points:**
- Data is displayed but insights and recommendations are limited
- Users know they should do something but don't know what
- Personalized guidance is the gap between "interesting data" and "behavior change"
- High-intent users (saving for a home, paying off debt) need more than just tracking

## Emerging Opportunities

### AI-Powered Financial Insights

**What users want:**

**1. Proactive Spending Alerts**
- "Tell me when I'm about to go over budget, not after I already did"
- "Catch unusual spending before I miss it — like a charge I didn't recognize"
- "Surface the things I should know, even if I didn't think to ask"

**2. Personalized Recommendations**
- "Based on my spending, tell me where I have room to save more"
- "Help me figure out which subscriptions I'm barely using and could cancel"
- "Tell me what I'd need to change to hit my savings goal in 6 months"

**3. Natural Language Questions**
- "Let me just ask: 'How much did I spend on food last month?'"
- "I want to be able to ask questions like I'd ask a person"
- "Don't make me navigate menus — just let me talk to my finances"

**4. Smarter Categorization**
- "Learn from my corrections and stop asking me to fix the same thing every month"
- "Let me describe a rule in plain language — 'anything from Amazon under $30 is Household'"
- "Predict what category something is before I have to touch it"

## Quantitative Data

From our survey of 500 FinTrack subscribers:

- **71%** say bank sync reliability is their top concern
- **64%** want investment account tracking (401k, brokerage, IRA)
- **58%** want AI-powered spending recommendations
- **52%** say they don't check the app regularly enough to stay on budget
- **47%** want proactive alerts when spending exceeds a category threshold
- **39%** say AI categorization errors are "frequent enough to be frustrating"

**Willingness to Pay for AI Features:**
- 74% would pay more for an AI spending coach that gave personalized recommendations
- Average willingness: additional $3–5/month on top of current subscription
- Segment most interested: debt payoff users (85%) and goal-based savers (78%)

---

## User Personas Most Affected

### 1. Goal-Oriented Savers (Jordan)
- High motivation to change behavior, low visibility into what to change
- Want the app to close the gap between data and action
- Will pay for coaching — not just tracking

### 2. Busy Families (The Hendersons)
- Too much going on to manually maintain budgets
- Need automation that doesn't require daily attention
- Joint visibility is a blocker to adoption in this segment

### 3. Debt Payoff Users (Alex)
- Most emotionally engaged with their finances
- Need clarity + motivation — tracking alone isn't enough
- Small wins and progress visibility drive retention in this segment

## Competitive Context

**What competitors are doing:**

- **Monarch Money:** Launched AI transaction notes and conversational budget summaries (Nov 2024)
- **Copilot:** Strong AI categorization with user rule-setting; investment tracking a core differentiator
- **Empower:** Proactive net worth and portfolio alerts; cash flow analysis built in
- **YNAB:** Launched in-app coaching content; strong community but no AI features yet

**Risk:** If FinTrack doesn't close the AI insights and investment tracking gap, high-value subscribers will move to Copilot or Empower for the complete picture.

**Opportunity:** FinTrack's combination of clean UX, broad account sync, and competitive pricing gives it a strong platform to deliver AI insights — if the categorization accuracy and reliability foundation is solid first.

---

## Recommendations for PRD Authors

When writing PRDs for AI spending insights or investment features:

1. **Reliability before intelligence**
   - Users won't trust AI recommendations if the underlying data has sync gaps or categorization errors
   - Fix bank sync reliability and categorization accuracy before adding AI layers

2. **Proactive, not passive**
   - The highest-value AI moments are when users *don't* open the app but still get value
   - Push notifications, email digests, and in-app alerts outperform passive dashboards for behavior change

3. **Start with high-confidence use cases**
   - Subscription detection, recurring bill alerts, and unusual spending are easier to get right than full spending coaching
   - Early wins build trust for more ambitious features

4. **Personalize to life stage and goals**
   - A debt payoff user and a first-time homebuyer saver need different insights
   - Goal-based context makes recommendations feel relevant rather than generic

5. **Plain language output**
   - Users don't want financial jargon — they want clear, specific, actionable language
   - "You spent $340 more on dining than last month — that's 3 fewer hours of freelance work" > "Dining spend increased 28% MoM"

---

**Remember:** FinTrack users aren't looking for more data — they're looking for clarity. Every AI feature should reduce the cognitive load of understanding your finances, not add to it. The goal is: open the app, immediately know what matters, know what to do next.
